Galvanic / Voltaic Cell

Spontaneous reaction → produces electrical energy

  • Chemical → Electrical energy
  • No external power source
  • Two half-cells + salt bridge
  • e.g. Batteries, Leclanche cell

Electrolytic Cell

Non-spontaneous ← requires electrical energy

  • Electrical → Chemical energy
  • External battery/DC required
  • Both electrodes in same beaker
  • e.g. Electroplating, refining
The Golden Rule — Never Changes
ANODE
Oxidation
Oxidation always occurs here — loss of electrons (OIL).
Galvanic: negative (−)  |  Electrolytic: positive (+)
CATHODE
Reduction
Reduction always occurs here — gain of electrons (RIG).
Galvanic: positive (+)  |  Electrolytic: negative (−)

OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain

RED CAT, AN OX — Reduction at Cathode, Oxidation at Anode

Galvanic / Voltaic Cells

Galvanic Cells

Spontaneous redox — the chemistry does the work

Identifying anode vs cathode

The metal with the more negative E° value (stronger reducing agent, lower on SRT) is oxidised → anode.

Stronger reducing agent (lower E°) → ANODE — oxidised, negative in galvanic Weaker reducing agent (higher E°) → CATHODE — reduced, positive in galvanic
DBE Example — Mg/Cu Cell (Q8.1 type)

Electrons flow left → right ∴ left electrode = anode. Mg E° = −2.37 V < Cu E° = +0.34 V. Mg is the stronger RA.

LabelNameMaterial / SolutionReason
(i)Anode / Negative electrodeMagnesium (Mg) rodStronger RA — oxidised
(ii)Cathode / Positive electrodeCopper (Cu) rodWeaker RA — reduced
(iii)Anode solutionMgSO₄(aq) — 1 mol·dm³Mg²⁺ product ions
(iv)Cathode solutionCuSO₄(aq) — 1 mol·dm³Cu²⁺ reactant ions
⚠️ Na₂CO₃ CANNOT be used — forms precipitates with Mg²⁺ and Cu²⁺, blocking ion flow.
Half-reactions
Anode
Oxidation
Mg(s) → Mg²⁺(aq) + 2e−
Mg solid dissolves — anode loses mass
Cathode
Reduction
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e− → Cu(s)
Cu deposits — cathode gains mass
Overall: Mg(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Mg²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)
Salt Bridge — Q8.4 (1 mark)

To maintain electrical neutrality in both half-cells by allowing ions to migrate between them.

Without the salt bridge, charge accumulates and stops the reaction. Anions migrate → anode cell; cations migrate → cathode cell.
Cell Notation
Mg(s) | Mg²⁺(aq) ‖ Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)
| = phase boundary  ·  ‖ = salt bridge  ·  Left = anode  ·  Right = cathode

Electrolytic Cells

Electrolytic Cells

Forcing non-spontaneous reactions with electrical energy

Definition — Electrolyte (2 marks)
Mark 1: A substance that dissociates into ions in solution (or when molten)
Mark 2: and therefore conducts electricity
Copper Refining
ANODE (+)
Impure Cu
Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e−
Oxidised — impure copper dissolves
CATHODE (−)
Pure Cu
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e− → Cu(s)
Reduced — pure copper deposits

Electrolyte: CuSO₄(aq)

⚠️ Precious metals (Ag, Au) are WEAKER reducing agents than Cu — NOT oxidised at anode. They sink as anode sludge.
Electroplating — Iron Rod with Copper
ANODE (+)
Electrode A
Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e−
Connected to + terminal. Cu dissolves.
CATHODE (−)
Iron rod
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e− → Cu(s)
Object being plated. Cu deposits.
Solution X = Cu(NO₃)₂ — copper(II) nitrate, because we need Cu²⁺ ions for plating.
When Ag Rod Replaces Electrode A
  • Cu²⁺ — already in solution from Cu(NO₃)₂
  • Ag⁺ — from Ag anode dissolving: Ag(s) → Ag⁺(aq) + e−
Ag⁺ plates preferentially — stronger oxidising agent than Cu²⁺ (higher on SRT) → more easily reduced at cathode.
Moles of Electrons Calculation — 1.6 g Cu deposited
  1. n(Cu): m ÷ M = 1.6 ÷ 63.5 = 0.0252 mol
  2. Ratio: Cu²⁺ + 2e− → Cu  ∴ 2 mol e− per mol Cu
  3. n(e−): 0.0252 × 2 = 0.0504 mol
  4. N(e−): 0.0504 × 6.02×10²³ = 3.03×10²² electrons

Worked Example — Finding Unknown Metal X (Q8.7)
  1. Write: E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode
  2. Cl₂ half-cell E° = +1.36 V. Cell emf = 2.10 V given.
  3. If Cl₂ is cathode: 2.10 = 1.36 − E°(X) → E°(X) = 1.36 − 2.10 = −0.74 V
  4. Find −0.74 V on SRT → closest is Zn (−0.76 V)
  5. Metal X = Zinc (Zn)
